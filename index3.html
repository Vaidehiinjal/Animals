The Cape Fur Seals of Cape Town

About the Cape Fur Seals

The largest and most muscular members of the fur sea family are Cape fur seals, commonly known as South African seals or brown seals (more on that later). They are technically separated into two subspecies: A. p. pusillus, which is larger and found along the southern African coast, and A. p. doriferus, which is smaller and found along the Victorian coast and in Tasmania. Another distinction between the two populations is that South African seals consume primarily fish, whereas Australian seals eat primarily squid. Around two million Cape fur seals are thought to live around the southern African shores, usually in 25-40 colonies on rocky coasts and islands; if you encounter a seal in South Africa, it is almost probably one of these. Cape fur seals, like all other seals, are semi-aquatic mammals that hunt in the sea and spend their time on land caring for their young, resting, recovering, and moulting. They are at a high risk of encountering human-caused threats such as fishing gear, boats, hunters, and plastic waste because of their semi-aquatic lifestyle. Cape fur seals are not "true seals". Officially known as "eared seals" or "otariids", fur seals differ from true seals in that their ears have external flaps, they have large fore flippers, and their hind flippers are not connected to the pelvis and can be rotated forward. These adaptations give eared seals the ability to walk and gallop on land, hear the direction of sound in air, and swim and manoeuvre much more powerfully than true seals.
