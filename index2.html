Himalayan Tahr of Table Mountain

About the Himalayan Tahr

Have you ever spotted a strange goat-like creature trotting the Table Mountain slopes? If so, you've come upon the Himalayan Tahr (Hemitragus jemlahicus), a big ungulate (hoofed animal) native to Northern India, Tibet, China, and Nepal. The tale of how these exotic "wild goats" ended up on Table Mountain is a fascinating one. The Groote Schuur Zoo was founded in the 1930s on the slopes of Table Mountain near the University of Cape Town. It spanned two hectares of land and housed a variety of species, including lions, crocodiles, emus, and tahrs, two of which escaped. A number of factors contributed to the Himalayan Tahr's success as an invasive species when it was roaming free.To begin with, Table Mountain and the tahr's original Himalayan habitat - steep, stony mountain cliffs bordered by woods and scrub – are strikingly similar. Second, the lack of predators on Table Mountain (in comparison to the Snow Leopard's original region) has meant that tahr population growth has been constrained primarily by access to food and water, as well as the animal's own reproductive rate. The tahr's versatile digestive system, which allows it to eat a wide variety of vegetation, is a third contributing element.
